# Fraud Case File #01 — Wirecard AG

**Type:** Internal Fraud / Accounting Manipulation / Insider Threat  
**Country:** Germany  
**Year:** 2020  
**Amount:** €1.9 billion missing  
**Sources:** Financial Times, Harvard Business School, Europol

---

## 1. Case Summary

Wirecard was a German fintech company listed on the DAX 30 — the index tracking Germany's 30 largest corporations. For years, it was celebrated as the pride of the German tech industry, reaching a peak valuation of over €24 billion.

In June 2020, the company announced that €1.9 billion supposedly held in escrow accounts in the Philippines had simply never existed. Three weeks later, Wirecard filed for insolvency. It remains the largest post-war financial scandal in German history.

---

## 2. Attack Flow

**Step 1 — Building the Facade**  
Wirecard created fictitious partners across Asia using intermediaries known as Third-Party Acquirers (TPAs). These entities were supposedly generating revenue on Wirecard's behalf in hard-to-access markets. To support this fiction, executives produced fabricated escrow account statements mimicking genuine accounts at Philippine banks BPI and BDO.

**Step 2 — Accounting Manipulation**  
Executives artificially inflated revenue and profits across financial reports. The missing €1.9 billion was presented as cash held in these offshore escrow accounts — accounts that never existed.

**Step 3 — Neutralizing Red Flags**  
When the Financial Times raised concerns as early as 2015, Wirecard retaliated legally, accusing the journalists of market manipulation. German regulators (BaFin) followed suit — investigating the reporters rather than the company itself.

**Step 4 — Collapse**  
In April 2020, KPMG, appointed for a special independent audit, announced it could not verify the existence of the funds. When contacted directly, BPI and BDO denied any relationship with Wirecard and confirmed that documents presented to EY auditors for years were crude forgeries. Wirecard filed for bankruptcy in June 2020.

**Step 5 — Flight**  
Chief Operating Officer Jan Marsalek fled before the official investigation even opened. He remains on Interpol's wanted list, believed to be sheltering in Russia.

---

## 3. Psychological Techniques

**Groupthink**  
Wirecard was the star of the German fintech scene. Investors, regulators, and auditors all wanted to believe in its success. Questioning Wirecard meant questioning Germany's national tech pride — a socially uncomfortable position to take.

**Halo Effect**  
Because Wirecard was high-tech, modern, and disruptive, stakeholders automatically assumed its leaders were visionary and trustworthy. This aura masked serious ethical blind spots and made skepticism feel like an attack.

**Confirmation Bias**  
Red flags had been surfacing since 2015. Yet whenever doubts emerged, stakeholders unconsciously sought evidence confirming what they already believed — that Wirecard was legitimate.

**Authority and Institutional Prestige**  
DAX 30 membership granted automatic credibility. Few could imagine that a company of this scale, audited by a Big Four firm, could sustain fraud on such a massive scale.

**Victim Reversal (DARVO)**  
When FT journalists began asking difficult questions, Wirecard sued them. This aggressive counter-attack successfully deflected public scrutiny for several years.

---

## 4. Technical Indicators

Warning signs were present but systematically ignored:

- Revenue heavily concentrated in regions difficult to audit independently (Dubai, Southeast Asia) through opaque TPA intermediaries
- A complex business model that effectively discouraged external analysts from asking deeper questions
- Revenue growth disproportionate to industry peers
- Escrow funds never directly verified with the foreign banks holding them
- External auditor EY reappointed year after year without rotation, validating financials based on third-party documents from Singapore rather than direct bank confirmations

---

## 5. Detection Opportunities

**Bank Confirmation Procedure:** Standard audit protocols require direct bank-to-bank confirmation of funds. EY bypassed this entirely — a direct call to BPI and BDO would have exposed the fraud years earlier. This was the central procedural failure of the case.

**Auditor Rotation:** EY had been auditing Wirecard for years without interruption. Mandatory rotation would have introduced fresh, unbiased scrutiny.

**Whistleblower and Press Signals:** FT journalists had been reporting anomalies since 2015. Treating their findings as credible intelligence rather than hostile attacks would have changed the outcome entirely.

**Independent TPA Audits:** Third-party intermediaries should have been subject to regular independent verification, not unconditional trust.

---

## 6. Prevention

- Mandatory external auditor rotation every 5 to 7 years
- Systematic enforcement of the Bank Confirmation procedure for all offshore funds
- Independent verification and auditing of Third-Party Acquirers
- Stronger whistleblower protection frameworks to encourage internal reporting
- Stricter European fintech regulation — an outcome the Wirecard scandal directly accelerated

---

## 7. Lessons Learned

> *"The most dangerous fraud is not the one hiding in the shadows. It is the one everyone refuses to see because it disrupts a comfortable narrative."*

The Wirecard case demonstrates that technology alone cannot detect fraud. Human biases — groupthink, the halo effect, institutional prestige — allowed €1.9 billion to vanish in plain sight, under the watch of regulators, auditors, and investors alike.

For any fraud analyst, the lesson is twofold: numbers can be manipulated, but human behavior leaves patterns. And audit procedures mean nothing if no one has the courage to actually enforce them.
